The gender pay gap is a measurement of the wage disparity between men and women in the United States.
The gender wage gap is based on separate data sets and data science undertaken by numerous reputable entities.
The salary disparity between men and women repeats year after year, demonstrating that the reasons for the pay
disparity are systematic and reflect how society appreciates women's labor less than men's.

The roots of the gender pay gap, according to research, are systemic, meaning that they arise from people's
judgments — conscious or unconscious — of the worth of women's labor versus men's work, and the types of employment
that women are suited for. Women are typically channeled into lower-paying roles as a result of these attitudes, with
the assumption that they do not have to work or that the work they perform should be related to childcare, homemaking,
and nurturing. It's also worth mentioning that labor labeled "women's work" is regarded as less important unless males
work in those fields. Women were, for example, the primary "computers," but computing jobs paid little until men entered the area.
