The roots of the gender pay gap, according to research, are systemic, meaning that they arise from people's
judgments — conscious or unconscious — of the worth of women's labor versus men's work, and the types of employment
that women are suited for. Women are typically channeled into lower-paying roles as a result of these attitudes, with
the assumption that they do not have to work or that the work they perform should be related to childcare, homemaking,
and nurturing. It's also worth mentioning that labor labeled "women's work" is regarded as less important unless males
work in those fields. Women were, for example, the primary "computers," but computing jobs paid little until men entered the area.

What we have created is a scatterplot. The first drop-down menu is showing data points for each gender.
You have the choice to either filter and view male or female data. The second and third dropdown menus both change their x
and y-axis to compare the different values in our data frame for example we have age, evaluation, seniority, salary, and bonus amount.
Our drop-down menus give you the option to compare and contrast all four values in whatever pairing as well as filtering out the gender.

In terms of closing the wage gap, there has been substantial progress. Not only do women earn almost as much as men do,
but in some sectors, they even earn more. Men make more in five of the ten job titles in the sample on average, while women make more in five.
As a Marketing associate and a Software Engineer, there is a significant wage disparity between men and women. As a Data Scientist and a
Warehouse Associate, men earn around $6,000 less than women.

Through this project, we have learned a more in-depth understanding of the gender pay discrepancy in the United States of America.
Our three main takeaways are:
- Men receive more compensation for their work not only based on bonus amount but as well as salary base pay.
- We found a commonality between the genders, primarily their pay rate increasing through age.
- And lastly, we found that even with regards to other hiring/promotion factors like seniority, age, and evaluation men
still receive a higher amount than women even with equal qualifications.


Although the existence of the gender pay gap is evidence of gender biases it is promising to see that throughout the years the gap is
decreasing and can be dismantled.
